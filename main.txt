\begin{abstract}
In low-power wide-area network (LPWAN) protocols (such as LoRaWAN), orthogonal $q$-ary modulation is often employed \changed{due to}{because of} its high capacity.
It is well established that $q$-ary coded modulation (CM) operates close to the Shannon limit at very low SNR. In this scheme, symbols from a non-binary (NB) error correction code defined over GF($q$) are directly mapped to a set of $q$ orthogonal modulation symbols. However, the high decoding complexity for large alphabet sizes ($q \in [64 \;  1024]$) has so far prevented practical adoption. This \changed{paper}{study} presents the first high-throughput software decoder for non-binary polar (\nbp{}) codes.
We introduce the Dual-Probability-Frequency Fast Successive Cancellation (DPF-FSC) decoding algorithm, which operates jointly in probability and frequency domains to reduce \added{the} computational complexity. Combined with graph pruning techniques and optimized for parallel execution on multicore SIMD architectures, our implementation achieves real-time performance suitable for software-defined radio (SDR) and cloud-RAN systems. In particular, we demonstrate that our proposed DPF-FSC decoder achieves throughputs ranging from several Mbps on low-power embedded processors to over 500 Mbps on modern multicore CPUs.
The most optimized implementation (\DecGener{}) reaches up to 900 Mbps on high-end processors for codes over \GF{16} \added{with $256$ symbols and a $20\%$ coding rate} in single-core configuration.
\changed{Even if}{ The throughput is reduced for} larger Galois fields (\GF{64} and \GF{256}) \deleted{use has a significant impact on the decoding throughput}, \added{but can still reach throughputs 40 to 400 Mbps, respectively, depending on the coding rate}.
\deleted{in line with the computational complexity increase), the decoders still achieve high throughput levels, especially in multicore configuration..}

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}


\IEEEPARstart{L}{ong-range}, low-power wireless communication systems have become a cornerstone of the rapidly expanding \iot{} ecosystem.
Their ability to provide reliable connectivity over wide geographical areas while maintaining extremely low-energy consumption makes them particularly attractive for applications such as smart metering, environmental monitoring, precision agriculture, and industrial automation.
Among the available technologies, the LoRaWAN protocol \cite{Aug16} has emerged as one of the most widely adopted standards for \lpwanpl{} \changed{due to}{ because} its scalability, robustness, and ease of deployment.
Other orthogonal modulation techniques targeting \iot{} applications also exist in the literature, including Golden modulation \cite{Van24} and Quasi-Cyclic Short Packet \cite{QCSP2022}, among others. 

A fundamental building block of such systems is the use of orthogonal $q$-ary modulation schemes, which enable efficient exploitation of the available spectrum.
These schemes allow, for example, the transmission of $p$ bits per symbol by mapping them onto one of $q = 2^p$ orthogonal sequences $p \in \mathbb{N}\backslash0$. In this context, it is well known that coded modulation significantly outperforms conventional Binary Interleaved Coded Modulation (BICM) \cite{Cair98}, especially in the low-SNR regimes typical of \lpwanpl{}. In this context, it is well established that coded modulation significantly outperforms conventional Binary Interleaved Coded Modulation (BICM) \cite{Cair98}, particularly in the low-SNR regimes characteristic of \lpwanpl{}. A gain of 2 dB was reported in \cite{Liva17}, enabling both improved transmission performance and enhanced spectrum utilization. However, this gain is achieved by replacing energy- and throughput-efficient binary codes with complex non-binary codes.
Indeed, achieving optimal performance requires non-binary \eccpl{} defined over a field that matches the modulation alphabet size, such as \nbldpc{}~\cite{Bar03} or \nbp{} codes~\cite{Par12}. These high-cardinality non-binary codes typically entail high decoding complexity and substantial memory requirements, which are at odds with the stringent computational and energy constraints of \iot{} transmission.

Bourduge et al.~\cite{Bou23} \deleted{have} proposed a novel solution based on hierarchical, small-cardinality non-binary modulations that circumvent complexity issues at the cost of a small sub-optimality, however implementing efficient real-time non-binary decoders remains challenging.

Over the past decade, to address flexibility issues in communication systems, research has focused on the effective implementation of \ecc{} decoders on multicore and manycore platforms~\cite{Kum13,Nik22} for Cloud-RAN contexts.
Decoders are a bottleneck for receivers, and implementing them efficiently in multicore or manycore platforms is not straightforward because they offer different parallelization schemes than ASICs or FPGAs. 
Consequently, decoding algorithms have been revisited, parallelized, and efficiently implemented on various programmable platforms \cite{Fal11,Fal16,LeG14,LeG15,Gia18,LeG19,Han17,Liu22,LeG24}.
Research focused on binary \eccpl{} widely used in 4G, 5G, and satellite standards.
Few efforts have been made to develop decoders for non-binary codes.
The use of NB-Low Density Parity Check (\nbldpc{}) codes in the CCSDS and BeiDou standards has generated a limited number of studies, demonstrating reduced throughput compared to binary code decoders.
To date, the decoding of \nbp{} codes has not been addressed, despite their promising potential in the context of \iot{} communications.

Polar codes, introduced by Arikan as the first class of provably capacity-achieving codes under low-complexity decoding \cite{Ari09}, have been successfully extended to non-binary alphabets \cite{Sav22}.
Complementing this, Cochachin et al. recently showed that, for $q$-ary orthogonal modulation, the kernel of an \nbp{} code can be further simplified  \cite{Coc23}.
The state of the art in hardware decoding of \nbp{} codes remains quite sparse, with variants of the extended min-sum algorithm and its simplified versions \cite{Far25, Coc21, Jab23}.
Notably, no efficient parallelized software implementation has yet been proposed.
In this study, we propose a lightweight, high-cardinality \nbp{} decoder that is efficiently parallelizable in software on multicore devices.

This work presents the first high-throughput software \scl{} decoder for \nbp{} codes.
Our primary contribution is a novel algorithm called the \fdpfsc{}.
The \fdpfsc{} operates jointly in the probability and frequency domains to reduce computational complexity.
This core algorithmic innovation is combined with the graph pruning techniques presented in \cite{Far25}.
Moreover, the decoding algorithms were optimized and adapted to leverage the parallelization features available in the current \simd{} multicore processing units.
A source code generation strategy is also employed to maximize the throughput.
The proposed implementations offer an attractive performance-complexity trade-off for high-order $q$-ary modulation in \lpwanpl{} for \sdr{} and cloud-RAN systems.
The experimental results demonstrate decoding throughputs ranging from several tens of Mbps on low-end systems (e.g., ARM A72 processor) to nearly 1 Gbps on general-purpose CPUs.


The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section \ref{sec:related_work} reviews related work, covering the principles of $q$-ary coded modulation based on orthogonal sequences and the fundamentals of \nbp{} encoding and decoding.
Section \ref{sec:FDPF SC} presents our proposed \fdpfsc{} decoding algorithm and analyzes its computational complexity.
Section \ref{sec:parallel} details the software parallelization and optimization strategies for the multicore \simd{} architectures.
Section \ref{sec:exp} provides comprehensive experimental results.
Finally, Section \ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper and outlines future research directions.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\nbp{} codes have been identified as powerful error correction codes, particularly in the context of \iot{} devices \cite{Yuan18,Elt25}. In \cite{Sav22}, Savin proposed combining \ccsk{} modulation with \nbp{} defined over a Galois field (GF) to transmit small payloads at very low \snrpl{}. The study demonstrates that the proposed coded-modulation scheme achieves a performance close to the achievable limits in the finite block-length regime. In \cite{Coc21}, it was shown that for \ccsk{} modulation, the multiplicative factor of the $2\times2$ kernel can be omitted without affecting the performance.
In this section, we summarize the principle of $q$-ary coded modulation and recall the demodulation principles.

\subsection{Principle of a $q$-ary Coded modulation}

The principle of $q$-ary coded modulation over the \GF{} of cardinality $q = 2^p$, denoted \GF{q}, is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:coded_modulation}. A message $\mathbf{M}$ of $Kp$ bits is first partitioned into $K$ symbols over \GF{q}, with each $q$ symbol represented by a binary vector of size $p$ bits, or as an integer between 0 and $q-1$. The $K$ symbols enter the \nbp{} encoder of rate $R = \frac{K}{N}$ to generate a codeword $\mathbf{C} = (c_0,\; c_1,\; \ldots,\; c_{N-1})$ of length  $N = 2^n$. The $q$-ary modulation maps each element $c \in \mGF{q}$ to a length-$q$ sequence $\pi(c)$ from the modulation set $\mathcal{C}_q$.
Each codeword symbol $c_i$ is thus mapped to the complex sequence $x_i = \pi(c_i)$, forming the transmitted frame $\mathbf{X}$ of length $Nq$. 
After transmission in an \awgn{} channel, the symbols of the noisy received frame $\mathbf{Y}$ are demodulated to generate a set of $N$ probability vectors, which are fed to the \nbp{} decoder. 
The decoder then decodes the message $\hat{\mathbf{M}}$. 
The following subsections detail the $q$-ary modulation/demodulation and \nbp{} coding and decoding processes.

\subsection{$q$-ary modulation and associated demodulation}

Let $\mathcal{C}_q$ denote a set of $q$ orthogonal sequences of length $q$ in $\mathbb{C}^q$ (the set of complex $q$-tuples), such that for any pair $u, v \in \mathcal{C}_q$, the inner product satisfies $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$ if $u \neq v$ and $\langle u, v \rangle = q$ if $u = v$, where $\langle u, v \rangle$ denotes the complex inner product of $u$ and $v$ .
For $q = 2^p$, an element $\alpha \in \mGF{q}$ (carrying $p$ bits of information) can be transmitted by selecting the sequence $\pi(\alpha) \in \mathcal{C}_q$, where $\pi$ is a bijective mapping between \GF{q} and $\mathcal{C}_q$. 
%With $q = 2^p$, it is possible to transmit an element $\alpha$ of GF($q$) ($p$ bit of information), by selecting the sequence $\pi(\alpha) \in \mathcal{C}_q$, with $\pi$ a mapping function between the set GF($q$) and $\mathcal{C}_q$.
Let $x = (x_0,\; x_1,\; \ldots,\; x_{q-1})$ be a sequence of $\mathcal{C}_q$ transmitted over an \awgn{} channel.

The received signal $y$ is thus given by $y = (y_0,\; y_1,\; \ldots,\; y_{q-1})$, with the $k^{\text{th}}$ term $y_k$ given as $y_k = x_k + \sigma w_k$, where $w_k$ is an independent realization of a normalized, zero-mean complex Gaussian random variable, and $\sigma^2$ is the complex noise variance.
In this channel, the \snr{} is defined as $\frac{1}{\sigma^2}$. 
 `	
Assuming equiprobable symbols and independent \awgn{} samples, $P(x|y)$ is given as 
\begin{align}
    P(x|y) =  & \frac{P(y|x)}{\sum_{x'\in \mathcal{C}_q}P(y|x')} \\
%                =& \frac{ \prod_{k=0}^{q-1} P(y_k| x_k)}{\sum_{x'\in \mathcal{C}_q}P(y|x') \prod_{k=0}^{q-1} P(y_k| x'_k)}  \\
%                =& \frac{  \exp\left( -\sum_{k=0}^{q-1} \frac{(y_k - x'_k)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)}{ \sum_{x'\in \mathcal{C}_q} \exp\left( -\sum_{k=0}^{q-1} \frac{(y_k - x'_k)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)} \\
                =& \frac{ \exp \left( \frac{\mathcal{R}(\langle y| x \rangle)}{\sigma^2} \right)}{\sum_{x'\in \mathcal{C}_q} \exp \left( \frac{\mathcal{R}(\langle y| x' \rangle)}{\sigma^2} \right)},
\label{eq:P(x|y)}  
\end{align}
with $\mathcal{R}(x)$ being the real part of $x$.
From the $i$th received symbol $y_i$, we define the probability vector $P^0_i = (P(\pi(\alpha)|y_i), \alpha \in \mGF{q})$. By definition, $\sum_{\alpha}P^0_i(\alpha)=1$.
Here, $P^0_i$ represents the demodulated symbol in the probability domain.
The optimal hard decision is
    \begin{equation}
        \hat{u} = \arg \underset{u \in \text{GF}(q)}{\max} P^0_i(u). 
        \label{eq:hard_decision}
    \end{equation}	
	
Several systems employ $q$-ary orthogonal modulation including:
Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) modulation \cite{Ber73} used in the LoRAWaN protocol \cite{Van17}, Zadoff-Chu sequences~\cite{Chu72} used in golden modulation~\cite{Van24}, or almost orthogonal \ccsk{}~\cite{CCSK}.
The modulation schemes encode data through a circular shift of the base sequence.
This circular encoding results in an efficient decoding algorithm because computing scalar products with a circularly shifted sequence reduces to circular convolution, which is performed efficiently in the frequency domain.
This reduces the computational complexity from $\mathcal{O}(q^2)$ to $\mathcal{O}(q \log_2 q)$ \cite{Sav22}. Finally, we note that all $q$-ary orthogonal coded modulations are equivalent in terms of mutual information. 

For LoRa modulation, a simpler, but equivalent, demodulation algorithm is usually applied. A LoRa symbol is modulated using a chirp with a frequency that varies linearly with time. Considering a given spreading factor SF, the $m^{th}$ symbol $x_m$ of the $2^{SF}$ dictionary codeword can be represented digitally by $x_m$, a vector of size $2^{SF}$ given as follows:
\begin{equation}
x_m(k) = e^{ 2\pi j \left( \frac{k^2}{2 \cdot 2^{\text{SF}}} - \frac{m k}{2^{\text{SF}}} \right)}, \: \: k = 0, 1, \dots, 2^{\text{SF}} - 1.
\label{eq:CSS}
\end{equation}

Let $y$ be the noisy-received version of a send codeword, (\ref{eq:P(x|y)}) implies the computation of the $2^{SF}$ scalar product $r(m) = <y|x_m>$ with

\begin{align}
r(m) &= \sum_{k=0}^{2^{SF}-1} y(k)e^{- 2\pi j \left(\frac{k^2}{2.2^{SF}} - \frac{m k}{2^{\text{SF}}} \right)},\\
     &= \sum_{k=0}^{2^{SF}-1} \left(y(k)e^{- 2\pi j \frac{k^2}{2.2^{SF}}}\right) e^{2\pi j \frac{m k}{2^{\text{SF}}} }\\
\label{eq:CSS_demos}
\end{align}

\noindent This is equal to the $m^{th}$ term of the Inverse Fast Fourier Transform (IFFT) of the terms by terms product of $y$ and $\text{conj}(x_0)$ multiplied by a factor $2^{SF}$. \textcolor{blue}{Thus, with coherent LoRA demodulation, $P(x_m|y)$ is expressed as}

\begin{revisionenv}
\begin{equation}
    P(x_m|y) = \frac{ \exp \left( \frac{\mathcal{R}(r(m))}{\sigma^2} \right)}{\sum_{m'\in \mathcal{C}_q} \exp \left( \frac{\mathcal{R}(r(m'))}{\sigma^2} \right)}.
\label{eq:P(x|y)_Lora}
\end{equation}
\end{revisionenv}


\subsection{\nbp{} encoding and decoding principles}

\subsubsection{Encoding principle}
Let $\mathbf{u} = (u_{0},\; u_{1},\; \dots,\; u_{N-1})$ be an input vector of length $N = 2^{n}$, where each element $u_{i}$ is a symbol in \GF{q} with binary decomposition $(u_{i,0},\; u_{i,1},\; \dots,\; u_{i,p-1}) \in \{0,1\}^p$. Subsequently, a \GF{q} element can also be represented by an integer between 0 and $q-1$.

The code is constructed by selecting the information set $\mathcal{I} \subset \{0, 1, \dots, N-1\}$ containing the indices of the $K$ most reliable virtual channels, with the complementary set $\mathcal{I}^c$ corresponding to the frozen positions.
The $K$ information symbols of $\mathbf{M}$ are placed in the positions of $\mathbf{u}$ indexed by $\mathcal{I}$ whereas the remaining positions are frozen positions set to the zero element of GF($q$). The method for determining $\mathcal{I}$ is described in \cite{Sav22}.
The fundamental kernel $\mathcal{T}$ generalizes Arikan's (Kronecker) kernel to \GF{\q{}}.
In its general form,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{T}:\ (u_{\theta}, u_{\phi}) \mapsto (c_{\theta}, c_{\phi}) = (u_{\theta} \oplus u_{\phi}, \beta u_{\phi}),
\end{equation}
where $\oplus$ denotes addition in \GF{q} (equivalent to bit-wise XOR for binary extension fields), and $\beta \in \mGF{q}$.
Following \cite{Coc23}, we set $\beta = 1$, the multiplicative identity in \GF{q}.
Encoding can be expressed as the matrix-vector multiplication $\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{G}_N$, with operations in \GF{q}.
Here, $\mathbf{G}_N$ is the $n$th Kronecker power of the fundamental kernel matrix $\mathbf{G}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$, thus $\mathbf{G}_N = \mathbf{G}_2^{\otimes n}$. 

The \scl{} decoder operates on the same factor graph as the encoder, but in reverse (see Figure~\ref{fig:polar_graph} for an example with $N=8$). 
\begin{figure}[!tb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{fig_N8_decoder1.png}
    \caption{Decoding graph of a $N=8$ \nbp{}. The variable nodes are indicated by an equality node}
    \label{fig:polar_graph}
\end{figure}
Layers are indexed by $\ell \in \{1,\dots,n\}$, with $\ell = n$ producing the source-symbol estimates on the left. 
The channel output is drawn at $\ell = 0$.
This is not a processing layer, but only the input to $\ell = 1$. 

\subsubsection{Decoding Principle}

We recall from \cite{Sav22} the equations for processing a $2 \times 2$ kernel during the decoding process.
As with binary polar codes, three processing functions are involved: the $f$-function in check nodes (CNs), the $g$-function in variable nodes (VNs) and the $h$-function during hard decision backward propagation.

Let $P_{\theta}^{\ell-1}$ and $P_{\phi}^{\ell-1}$ be the two probability vector inputs to a kernel at layer $\ell$.
These inputs are processed by the CN and VN to produce output probability vectors $P_{\theta}^{\ell}$ and $P_{\phi}^{\ell}$, which are then passed to layer $\ell+1$.
The update at a CN ($f$-function) is:
\begin{equation}
P_{\theta}^{\ell}(\alpha)
= \sum_{\beta \in \text{GF}(q)} \hspace{-.5em}
P_{\theta}^{\ell-1}\!\big(\alpha \oplus \beta\big)
\, P_{\phi}^{\ell-1}(\beta),
\; \forall \alpha \in \mGF{q}.
\label{eq:CN_prb}
\end{equation}

Similarly, the update at a VN ($g$-function) is given by:
\begin{equation}
P_{\phi}^{\ell}(\alpha)
= \eta_v^{-1} \, P_{\theta}^{\ell-1}\!\big( \hat{u}_{\theta}^{\ell} \oplus \alpha \big) 
\, P_{\phi}^{\ell-1} (\alpha),
\; \forall \alpha \in \mGF{q},
\label{eq:VN_prb}
\end{equation}
where $\eta_v^{-1}$ is a normalization factor that ensures that the probabilities sum to 1, i.e., $\eta_v =  \sum_{\alpha \in \text{GF}(q)} P_{\phi}^{\ell}\big(\alpha\big)$.
The estimated symbols across the decoding layers are obtained as follows:
\begin{equation}
(\hat{u}_{\theta}^{\ell-1}, \hat{u}_{\phi}^{\ell-1}) = 
(\hat{u}_{\theta}^{\ell} \oplus \hat{u}_{\phi}^{\ell},\; 
\hat{u}_{\phi}^{\ell}).
\label{eq:propagation_symbol}
\end{equation}

At the final stage (leaf), the hard decision for each symbol $\hat{u}_i^n$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
\hat{u}_i^n =
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } i \in \mathcal{I}^{c}, \\
\argmax[i]{P_i^n}, & \text{if } i \in \mathcal{I}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:hatu_in}
\end{equation}

Finally, to obtain the full symbol sequence $\hat{\bm{u}} = (\hat{u}_0^n,\; \ldots,\; \hat{u}_{N-1}^n)$, we sequentially apply \eqref{eq:CN_prb}, \eqref{eq:VN_prb}, and \eqref{eq:propagation_symbol} at each decoding stage $\ell$ on the tree structure (see Figure~\ref{fig:tree}.a).

The computational complexity of the decoder is dominated by the $\f$-function (see~\eqref{eq:CN_prb}), which requires $\q{}^2$ multiplications to be performed.
To relieve the computational burden, \cite{Jab23} proposed performing the $\f$-function in the log-likelihood domain using variants of the extended min-sum (EMS) algorithm.
Additionally, the $\g$-function (see~\eqref{eq:VN_prb}) requires a permutation operation, which is poorly suited for parallel software implementations.
Section~\ref{sec:FDPF SC} addresses these issues by processing the kernels in the frequency domain.
Before proceeding to this section, we describe the Fast \scl{} algorithm.


\subsection{Fast \scl{} NB decoder}
\label{sec:Fast SC non-binary decoder}
Recently, the fast \scl{} decoding technique for binary polar codes, originally presented in~\cite{Sar14}, was been extended to \nbp{}~\cite{Far25}.
This optimization strategy exploits the simplification opportunities offered by decoding blocks with specific frozen bit patterns. 
Among the simplification-enabling patterns proposed in~\cite{Far25}, we adopt the following four configurations: Rate-0, Rate-1, repetition node (REP), and Single Parity Check (SPC) patterns. 
As with binary polar codes, {Rate-0}, {Rate-1}, and {REP} pruning strategies provide results identical to those of subtree processing.
However, this was not the case for the {SPC} pattern.
This optimization can negatively impact the decoding performance because of the decoding approximation made to avoid complex trellis-based decoding.
Consequently, we quantified the negative impact of the {SPC} node decoding algorithm reported in~\cite{Far25} (Algorithm 1, page 6).
The frame error rate (FER) performance of the \nbp{} codes with $\N{} = 64$ and $1024$ for different code rates $\R{}$ and GF cardinality $\q$ is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:FER}.
In several contexts, the performance degradation induced by the SPC approximation was less than $0.1$~dB, which is considered acceptable.

\subsection{Algorithmic formulation}

In \cite{Declercq2010}, it was shown that for \nbldpc{} codes, the complexity of CN processing can be reduced from $\mathcal{O}(q^2)$ to $\mathcal{O}(q\log_2(q))$ by replacing the probability vectors convolution (see~\eqref{eq:CN_prb}) with a product in the Fourier domain. This method has been successfully used for software implementations of \nbldpc{} codes~\cite{And14,LeG20}.
Because the CN processing ($f$-function) of \nbp{} codes corresponds exactly to the processing of a degree-3 CN of \nbldpc{} (with only one extrinsic message computed, instead of 3), it is possible to process the CN ($f$-function) in the frequency domain for an \nbp{} decoder. 

In \GF{q}, the Fourier transform is expressed using the Hadamard matrix $\mathbf{H}_q$ as $F = \mathbf{H}_q P$, where $\mathbf{H}_q$ is the $p$-th Kronecker power (recall that  $p=\log_2(q)$) of the length-2 Fourier transform matrix
%$\mathbf{H}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$, i.e., $\mathbf{H}_q = \mathbf{H}_2^{\otimes p}$.
$\mathbf{H}_2 = [\; [\; 1,\; \phantom{-}1\; ]\; ;\; [\; 1,\; -1\; ]\; ]$, i.e., $\mathbf{H}_q = \mathbf{H}_2^{\otimes p}$.
The \fwht{} performs this operation in $p$ layers, with each layer involving $q$ addition/subtraction. This operation is denoted $F = \FWHT{P}$ in the following (the index $q$ is omitted for simplicity). By convention, $F$ will represent a vector in the frequency domain and $P$ represents a vector in the probability domain.

The \ifwht{} is expressed as $P = \IFWHT{F} = \frac{1}{q}\FWHT{F}$. Thus, equation \eqref{eq:CN_prb} can be replaced by
\begin{equation}
P_{\theta}^{\ell} = \frac{1}{q}\FWHT{\FWHT{P_{\theta}^{\ell-1}} \odot \FWHT{P_{\phi}^{\ell-1}}}.
\label{eq:CN_prb_freq_prd}
\end{equation}
It is interesting to keep the output of \eqref{eq:CN_prb_freq_prd} in the frequency domain by suppressing the last \ifwht{}, thereby directly computing the output in the frequency domain $F_{\theta}^{\ell} = \FWHT{P_{\theta}^{\ell}}$ as follows:
\begin{equation}
F_{\theta}^{\ell} =  \FWHT{P_{\theta}^{\ell-1}} \odot \FWHT{P_{\phi}^{\ell-1}}.
\label{eq:CN_prb_freq}
\end{equation}
This equation provides results in the frequency domain, rather than in the probability domain. This approach is justified because if $F_{\theta}^{\ell}$ is passed to layer $\ell + 1$ for CN processing, it remains in the frequency domain, thereby avoiding unnecessary back-and-forth transformations between the frequency and probability domains.

%Using the FWHT, the complexity of CN processing reduces to $3q\log_2(q)$ additions/subtractions (the 2 directs FWHT and the inverse FWHT), $2q$ products (the $q$ terms by terms multiplication $\odot$ and the $q$ multiplications by the constant $\frac{1}{q}$. 

Similarly, it is also possible to process part of equation \eqref{eq:VN_prb} in the frequency domain. Specifically, let $\hat{u}$ be a constant element of GF($q$). Let $P_{\hat{u}}$ be the probability distribution associated with $\hat{u}$, that is, a null vector except at index $\hat{u}$ where $P_{\hat{u}}(\hat{u}) = 1$. Consider the operation $\gamma = \alpha \oplus \hat{u}$,where $P_{\gamma}$, $P_{\alpha}$, and $P_{\hat{u}}$ are the probability distributions of the three variables. 

In the probability domain, $P_{\gamma}$ is obtained from $P_{\alpha}$ through a permutation, that is, $P_{\gamma}(\beta) = P_{\alpha}(\beta + \hat{u}), \: \forall \beta \in \mGF{q}$. However, in the Fourier domain, we obtain 
\begin{equation}
    P_{\gamma} = \frac{1}{q} \mathcal{H} (F_{\alpha} \odot F_{\hat{u}}),
    \label{eq:Pgamma}
\end{equation}
with $F_{\hat{u}} = \mathbf{H}_q P_{\hat{u}}$ equal to the $\hat{u}^{th}$ column of the Hadamard matrix $\mathbf{H}_q$. Because $F_{\hat{u}}$ is a vector of -1 and +1 elements, it can be almost seamlessly incorporated into the first stage of the \fwht{}, giving rise to a modified \fwht{} denoted $\mathcal{H}_{\hat{u}}$. Using this method, equation \eqref{eq:VN_prb} can be reformulated as follows:
\begin{equation}
P_{\phi}^{\ell} =  \eta_v^{-1]} \, P_{\phi}^{\ell-1} \odot \FWHT{}_{\hat{u}_\theta} ( F_{\theta}),
\label{eq:VN_prb_freq}
\end{equation}
where the normalization factor $\eta_v^{-1}$ incorporates the factor $\frac{1}{q}$ from \eqref{eq:Pgamma}.

In practice, even with floating-point arithmetic, the inverse Hadamard transform can generate near-zero or even small negative probability values owing to finite precision effects, particularly when subtracting nearly equal numbers. These spurious zeros, if left unclipped, can accumulate through successive decoding layers and eventually cause an error floor at high SNRs. To prevent this, we applied a simple clipping operation after every inverse transform: any probability value smaller than a threshold $\varepsilon = 10^{-10}$ is replaced by $\varepsilon$. The threshold $\epsilon$ is chosen sufficiently small to have no measurable impact on performance while being large enough to flush out underflowed values. Empirical results show that this clipping eliminates the error floor entirely up to a frame error rate of $10^{-8}$. In the following, we denote by $\bar{\mathcal{H}}$ the inverse Hadamard transform that includes clipping.

\section{Software parallelization strategy}
\label{sec:parallel}

Modern multicore processors deliver sufficient computational power to enable high-speed \sdr{} and Cloud-RAN systems. For binary error control codes, the reported throughputs range from hundreds of Mbps to multiple Gbps: see \cite{LeG15} for binary turbo codes, \cite{Fal16,LeG24} for binary LDPC codes, and \cite{Gia18} for binary polar codes.
Achieving similar decoding throughput with NB-codes is challenging due to higher computational complexity.
However, for \nbp{}, we demonstrate that such throughputs can be achieved using the proposed DPF-FSC algorithm, combined with a careful software implementation that fully leverages the capabilities of modern multicore processors \footnote{The source code will be released under a FOSS license online after publication.}.

\subsection{Decoding algorithm parallelization strategy}
%
%
%%%%%%%% FENCE CM

%The \nbsc{} decoding algorithm exposes several parallelization opportunities.
%Different parallelization strategies are applied in related works to exploit \ecc{} computation inherent parallelism and adequately implement decoding algorithms to match platform supported features.

Three main SIMD parallelization strategies can be applied to efficiently implement the proposed DPF-FSC algorithm. The first is word-level parallelism $\mathcal{S}_1$, where multiple independent codewords are decoded in parallel (\cite{LeG14,LeG15}, with $\Q > 1$). The second is node-level parallelism $\mathcal{S}_2$, where computations for multiple nodes are performed in parallel (see \cite{Cas15,Gia18}, which computes $\Q$ independent $\f$ or $\g$ function calls in parallel). Finally, the third level of parallelism is achieved within a single node $\mathcal{S}_3$. For binary codes, the third level is less relevant, and only levels $\mathcal{S}_1$ and $\mathcal{S}_2$ are typically employed. However, for NB codes, level $\mathcal{S}_1$ is the most natural starting point, because it offers both flexibility and low-latency decoding. This strategy has already been successfully applied to NB low-density parity-check (NB-LDPC) codes \cite{And13,LeG20}.

\subsection{Memory arrangement and SIMD implementation}
%

Efficient memory mapping and a low decoder memory footprint are crucial for achieving high performance on multicore targets.
Indeed, data mapping in memory enables manual or even automatic vectorization of computations.
The \fdpfsc{} decoding algorithm presented previously requires storing four main data sets: 1) the soft channel inputs, 2) the soft messages exchanged across decoding nodes, 3) decision symbols produced during graph traversal, and 4) the decoder output.
We now describe the memory requirements for each type of memory. because the SC scheduling is identical for binary polar codes and \nbp{}s, the memory optimization proposed for binary polar codes in~\cite{Ler13} can be directly applied to the NB-SC decoder.
Consequently, the channel information equals $N$ probability vectors containing each $q$ values. The memory required for internal message exchange is reduced to $N-1$ soft messages. In the \fdpfsc{} decoder, the exchanged messages can be either probability or frequency vectors. Each soft message is represented as either a probability vector $P_{\theta}^{\ell}$ or as a frequency vector $F_{\theta}^{\ell}$, each comprising $q$ floating-point values. because they are composed of $q$ elements of identical size, the memory space can be shared over time without problems. Thus, the memory complexity of the \fdpfsc{} decoder is identical to that probability-only decoders. Finally, the $N-1$ decision message corresponds to an element of $\mGF{q}$ encoded using $p$ information bits, such as the $N$ output symbol values. Note that during the decoding process, two memory buffers of $q$ elements are required during the probability-to-frequency conversion and vice-et-versatility.

Assuming that each element of a soft vector is encoded using the 32-bit IEEE floating-point format (4 octets), and that the $p$ bits encoding a Galois field element require $W = 1$ octet for $p \leq 8$ or $W = 2$ octets for $8 < p \leq 16$ (higher-order Galois fields are not considered in this study), the total memory footprint $M$ of a single decoder, expressed in octets, is given by

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    \M &= \underbrace{4qN}_{\textrm{input data}} + \underbrace{(4q + w)(N-1)}_{\textrm{message data}} + \underbrace{wN}_{\textrm{output data}} + \underbrace{2q}_{\textrm{buffers}} \\
      &= (8q + 2w)N - 2q - w \label{eq:M}
\end{split}
\end{equation}

The $\mathcal{S}_3$ parallelization strategy optimizes $\M$ to align with the memory cache capacity.
For instance, with $\N = 256$ and $q = 64$, $\M = 129$ kB, which fit within the CPU caches (see Table \ref{tab:platforms}). A more extensive overview of the memory complexity of the decoder, depending on its $N$ and $GF$ configurations is provided in Table \ref{tab:mem}. This highlights the fact that for small N and GG values, memory complexity can comply with microcontroller and IoT constraints.

\subsection{Decoding tree path processing}
%
In the previous sections, we described how the core decoding functions are parallelized.
This section focuses on the strategies for traversing the decoding trees.
Two approaches were investigated in this study.

The first approach, used in decoder \DecPbFq{}, relies on a recursive description of the tree traversal using homogeneous nodes adapted to handle the heterogeneous exchanged messages.
Input data are tagged by a bit indicating their nature (frequencies or probabilities). 
It is checked before the computation of outgoing messages by $\f$ and $\g$ functions, and conversions are applied accordingly.
It yields a uniform decoding description with a self-contained node class.
However, runtime conditional operations may disrupt the execution pipeline, resulting in time penalties.
The second approach, used in decoder \DecPbFqS{}, uses heterogeneous nodes.
They are specialized based on incoming messages nature.
Two node classes are defined: \textit{node after $\f$} (NaF) for $\theta(n+1-\ell) = 0$, and \textit{node after $\g$} (NaG) for $\theta(n+1-\ell)=1$, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:tree}.
The previously added information bit is not needed any more in this approach, which does not need the associated runtime conditional operations. related to domain conversion.
As a trade-off, it slightly increases the program memory footprint.

The impact of these different strategies on performance is evaluated in Section~\ref{sec:exp}.

\subsection{Integration of pruning optimization} \label{sec:parallel:pruning}
%
Decoding graph pruning~\cite{Far25} is highly dependent on parameters $\N{}$ and $\R{}$.
To provide different trade-offs between flexibility and efficiency, two pruning integration strategies were investigated.

The first strategy, which is used in decoder \DecPruned{}, dynamically prunes the graph at runtime.
During decoder initialization, a pruned decision tree is constructed based on the frozen-bit matrix and the parameters $\N{}$ and $\R{}$, defining each node's type (e.g., normal, rate-0, and so on).
The tree representation is converted into a decision vector used to select the next node type to process at each decoding step, i.e., the corresponding computations or functions to execute.
This approach allows for generic decoder software, at the expense of frequent runtime look-ups to select the next node class for all supported types.
This dynamic control flow can result in branch mispredictions and execution penalties.

To flatten the control-flow structures and suppress branch misprediction penalties, a second approach based on decoder-specific code generation was implemented, following principles similar to those used for binary SC decoders in~\cite{Sar14b}.
This strategy, used in decoder \DecGener{}, generates a specialized software description of the decoder directly from the pruned decoding tree for specific $\N{}$ and $\R{})$ parameters.
It also enables static optimizations that reduce decoding computational complexity,
such as omitting $\f$ and $\g$ function evaluation when the successor node is of type rate-0. %, an optimization difficult to achieve dynamically with \DecPruned{}.
To limit code size growth and reduce instruction cache miss risks at runtime, nodes of identical type are factorized using the approach in~\cite{Sar14b}.
This strategy executes only operations strictly required for decoding, improving performance at the cost of flexibility, and software needs to be generated for all different $\N$ and $\R$ parameters.

An evaluation of the impact of pruning and an analysis of the effects of the integration strategy are provided in Section~\ref{sec:exp}.

\subsection{Coarse-grained Parallelism Optimization}
%
Modern SIMD-capable processors integrate multiple physical cores and support logical multithreading.
To exploit this dimension of parallelism, we applied strategy $\mathcal{S}_1$ by running one independent decoder instance per core, as reported in~\cite{LeG15}.
Moreover, $\mathcal{S}_3$ has already been efficiently exploited by the SIMD structure of each processing core.
The Strategy $\mathcal{S}_2$, however, has drawbacks: at layer $\ell$, only $2^{n-\ell}$ kernels can be processed in parallel, and this fine-grained multithreading approach incurs substantial fork-join overheads, reducing potential performance gains.

In practice, the observed acceleration is lower because of contention on shared architectural resources such as memory bandwidth and the last-level (L3) cache, which increase access latency. 
Nevertheless, experimental results indicate that these penalties remain limited, and near-linear scalability is still achievable for a moderate number of parallel decoder instances. 
The relevance of this approach is illustrated in the experimental section, with measurements on platforms featuring 4 to 32 cores.

\subsection{Performance assessment of level-1 optimizations}
\label{sec:exp:level-1}

This section presents the throughputs achieved by the reference probability-based decoder, denoted \DecProba{}, and the decoder described in Section~\ref{sec:FDPF SC}, hereafter denoted \DecPbFq{}. 
This choice simplifies the analysis of throughput as a function of decoder parameters, whereas the acceleration provided by pruning strategies and the resulting decoders \DecPruned{} and \DecGener{} are discussed in the following subsection.
First, the coded throughput ($\R{} \times$ information one) was measured for frame lengths $\N{}$ ranging from $16$ to $1024$, with a coding rate $\R{} = 50\%$ and Galois field order $q = 64$.
The results are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:N}.

The first observable trend, albeit predictable, is that throughput decreases as $\N$ increases.
Indeed, processing more symbols requires more computations, increasing latency and thus reducing throughput.
The interesting aspect of this trend is that all implementations are affected nearly equally, demonstrating that the algorithmic refinement is relevant independently of $\N$ for given $\R$ and $\q$. 
Moreover, it seems that in single-threaded execution, \DecProba{} and \DecPbFqS{} have similar performances on $\Pl{2}$, $\Pl{3}$ and $\Pl{4}$, which indicates that the clock frequency is more important than the cache sizes for such values of $\R$ and $\q$.

\DecPbFq{} seems more impacted by the available features, behaving like \DecProba{} on $\Pl{1}$, as \DecPbFqS{} on $\Pl{3}$ and remaining in between on $\Pl{2}$ and $\Pl{4}$.
While several explanations could be proposed (pipeline sizes, better branch predictors, variable instruction length of x86\_64 CPUs), it highlights how the refined \DecPbFqS{} is the better approach to reach better throughputs independently of the target and of $\N$.


%%%% CAMILLE CONTINUERA LÀ
Next, we investigated the effect of $\q$ on the throughput.
Indeed, the complexity of the \fwht{} computation in the polar tree nodes depends on this factor.
To quantify this impact, experiments were conducted with $\q = 2^p$ and $p \in \llbracket 3, 8 \rrbracket$.
In these experiments, the $\R$ was fixed at $50\%$ ($1/2$) and the frame length at $\N = 64$.
The resulting coded throughputs are illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:GF}.


First, the results confirm that \DecPbFqS{} is superior to \DecProba{} and \DecPbFq{}, as it almost always outperforms them in terms of throughput. Two outliers can be observed on $\Pl{4}$ for $q = 8$ and $q = 16$ where \DecPbFq{} outperformed the other models. This results from the optimizations performed by the compiler (GCC 14) which took advantage of the systematic structure of \DecPbFq{}. This behavior is specific to this setup and is not replicated when $\N$ or $q$ is increased or when another compiler is used (e.g., Clang).
From $\q = 8$ to $64$, the throughput is relatively stable, falling by $1.5$x to $2$x while $\q$ doubles.
The program and associated data fit in their respective L1 caches, while the relative augmentation at the beginning can be explained by better use of \simd{} features.
However, from $\q = 128$, the negative slope appears to reach $-15\times$.
To explain this behavior, the cache miss rate and instructions per cycle (IPC) were measured using the Linux perf stat API on the three Linux-based platforms.
%They are reported in Table~\ref{TOBEADDED}.
The cache miss rate remains below $0.3\%$ on $\Pl{1}$, and even below $0.1\%$ for the other platform when $\q < 128$.
It then increases from $0.5\%$ to approximately $10\%$ for $q \geq 128$. \subsection{Performance of pruning-based decoders}
\label{sec:exp:level-2}
%
%
After verifying the impact of $\N$ and $\q$ on decoder throughput evolution, we aimed to quantify the gains from implementing the pruning optimizations described in Section~\ref{sec:FDPF SC}.
As described previously, four pruning optimizations were integrated: Rate-0, Rate-1, REP, and SPC.
A second series of experiments was conducted to validate their benefits.
Two types of decoders incorporating pruning-based optimizations were evaluated:
first, a generalist decoder, denoted \DecPruned{}, that adapts the applied pruning optimization on the fly, enabling a single compiled decoder to efficiently handle all polar codes regardless of $\N$ and $\R$.
Second, a decoder, denoted \DecGener{}, whose source code is generated and compiled ahead of time to maximize throughput performance for specific $\N$ and $\R$.
Supporting various decoding configurations thus requires multiple decoder \DecGener{} binaries.

Figure~\ref{fig:prune:mac} presents and compares the throughputs achieved by \DecPbFqS{}, \DecPruned{}, and \DecGener{} \nbp{} decoders on $\Pl{2}$.
In these experiments, the pair $(\N, \;\q)$ is fixed, and the coding rate $\R$ was taken in the interval $[20\%, 90\%]$ to apply different simplification schemes and evaluate different pruning configurations.
Three frame configurations were selected for benchmarking across all platforms, with $\N = 256$ and $\q = 16$, $64$, and $128$, shown in Figures \ref{fig:prune:mac}(a), (b), and (c), respectively.

First, it can be observed that the throughput of the \DecPruned{} decoder is, as expected, higher than that of the decoder without tree pruning (\DecPbFqS{}).
The throughput gains achieved with the \DecPruned{} decoder reach factors ranging from $2\times$ for the highest $\q$ at $\R \simeq 85\%$ to $5.7\times$ for the lowest $q$ at $R \simeq 19\%$. These gains vary significantly with the code rate.
Codes with lower rates benefit from substantial computation simplifications, yielding larger gains.
The most significant gains here are linked to the reduced computational complexity from Rate-0 and REP nodes at low code rates.
However, Rate-1 and SPC nodes, which dominate at high code rates, also provide substantial gains. Thus, if \DecPruned{} decoders are $1.75$x to $3$x slower than \DecGener{} for lower $\R$, this factor tends toward $0$ as $\R$ increases.
Both reach approximately $100$ Mbps for $\R \in [50\%, 70\%]$ and $q = 64$, and \DecGener{} reaches up to $800$ Mbps for $q = 16$, exceeding \iot{} requirements.


The final interesting result stems from the information throughput (ITh) comparisons.
For $\q = 64 = 2^6$, at $\R = 19\%$, \DecGener{} reaches an ITh of $400 \times 6 \R = 456$ Mbps and \DecPruned{} reaches $160 \times 6 \R = 182$ Mbps, while \DecPbFqS{} achieves $167$ Mbps for $\R = 90\%$.
This means that, based on Figure~\ref{fig:FER}, \snrpl{} of $-12 dB$ can be reached by \DecGener{} (reps. \DecPruned{}) with $3$x (resp. $1.2$x) the ITh achieved by \DecPbFqS{} at $\R  = 90\%$.
At $\R \simeq 75\%$, both pruned decoder reach an ITh $90 \times 6 \R = 405$ Mbps, thus still a $\sim3$x throughput, but now for \snrpl{} around $-6$ dB. 
These results suggest that if flexibility is not an issue \DecGener{} and low code rates should be preferred, whereas if higher code rates (i.e., lower \snrpl{}) are required, typically $\R > 80\%$, or if a single self-contained decoder program is required, \DecPruned{} should be chosen.

The final aspect to explore is how these different implementations scale across several cores, and their impact on energy efficiency.

\subsection{Evaluation of decoder scalability and efficiency}
\label{sec:exp:scalable}

In the previous subsections, we focused on the decoder performance when operating on a single physical core ($\M=1$) of multicore architectures that include up to $32$ cores in our setups.
In this section, we evaluate and demonstrate the scalability of the proposed parallel decoders for multicore systems.
To achieve this, we measured the maximum achievable throughput on each platform, with a distinct decoder instance per physical core. For practical reasons, all parallel decoder instances have the same parameters ($\N$, $\R$, and $\q$), though this is not required.

A summary of the experimental results is presented in Figure~\ref{m:cores}.
This figure presents the acceleration factors obtained when $\M$ physical cores are used.
To mitigate the effect of clock frequency scaling, programs were run for $60$s.

Analyzing the results from the constrained platform $\Pl{1}$, the throughput with 4 active cores is typically $\approx4\times$ higher, scaling linearly with the core count.
For the other platforms, average acceleration factors of $7.5\times$, $4.2\times$, and $27.6\times$ are observed on $\Pl{2}$, $\Pl{3}$, and $\Pl{4}$, respectively.
For $\Pl{3}$ and $\Pl{4}$, the gap between the number of active cores and acceleration factor is explained by the clock frequency difference, which is approximately $1.2\times$ (resp. $1.5\times$) lower when all cores are active.
For $\Pl{2}$,  the scaling is nearly equal to $8\times$, and the difference may stem from synchronization overheads.

With coded throughput exceeding $1$ Gbps on platform $\Pl{2}$ and reaching up to $5$ Gbps on $\Pl{4}$ on \GF{16}, or even over $3$ Gbps for low rates on \GF{128}, the parallel decoders achieve throughput that scales nearly linearly with the number of physical cores employed.
This demonstrates the scalability of this approach across all platforms.

Finally, the energy consumption was estimated.
We used the average power drawn by each platform in single- and multicore operations, as reported in Table~\ref{tab:platforms}, and divided it by the coded throughput achieved.
The results are reported in $\mu$J per coded bit as bar graphs in figure~\ref{fig:energy}, for \DecPruned{} and \DecGener{} for a coding rate $\R \in \{25\%, 75\%\}$, for symbols in \GF{64} and frame length $\N = 256$.
These settings correspond to those used for figure~\ref{fig:prune:mac}b, only reported for the two best implementations presented so far.

Those results clearly demonstrate that scalability translates to systematic reduction in energy consumption for multicore variants, independently of the coding rates or decoder variant in use.
%They also confirm that the rate is a predominant parameter,

An interesting result is that for $\R = 75\%$, \DecPruned{} and \DecGener{} yield nearly the same result.
As presented in subsection~\ref{sec:parallel:pruning}, they differ the most when computations can be omitted, such as before Rate-0 nodes.
Such occasions are rarer at higher code rates. This suggests \DecGener{} may be suitable for high code rate targets only if a restricted subset of settings can be defined ahead of time, while \DecPruned{} should be preferred otherwise in such cases on any platform.

However, for low code rates, \DecGener{} has a clear advantage over \DecPruned{}.
For $\R = 25\%$, \DecGener{} consumes 1.7x less energy per coded bit (due to a throughput 1.7x higher) than \DecPruned{}. The same can be said for $\Pl{2}$ and $\Pl{3}$, where the consumption is reduced by 2x.
On $\Pl{4}$, while a factor of 1.7x can be observed in the single-core, it falls to 1.1x in the multicore, the latter being the main use case for such platforms.
Thus, on $\Pl{4}$, unless the extra throughput performance enabled by \DecGener{} is required, \DecPruned{} should be preferred, enabling complete flexibility across the rate settings.

\subsection{Comparison with related works}

To the best of our knowledge, no software implementations dedicated to the decoding of NB-p codes have been reported in the literature, preventing a direct comparison. Nevertheless, several efficient software implementations have been developed, for instance, for binary polar code decoding \cite{Cas15,LeG14,Sar14,Gia18} and for non-binary LDPC codes \cite{Fer22,LeG20}, which represent the closest works to our contribution. It is important to emphasize that the non-binary polar decoders proposed in this work, which rely on the SC-FFT decoding algorithm, achieve higher error-correction performance than conventional binary polar decoders based on SC \cite{Cas16} and SC-SCAN \cite{LeG18}. However, this performance gain comes at the cost of a higher computational complexity, as discussed in Section I.

In the literature, binary SC decoders \cite{LeG14} have been shown to achieve very high throughput, exceeding 2 Gbps per core on platforms similar to P3 for code lengths $N<2048$ using a single processing core. However, such high throughput, which naturally results in a reduction in the energy consumption per decoded bit (by approximately one order of magnitude), is achieved at the expense of a degradation in the error-correction performance of approximately 2 to 3 dB.

In parallel, more powerful binary polar code decoders have been proposed in terms of error correction capability to partially compensate for SNR gaps. These decoders are typically based on the SCL algorithm \cite{Tal11}. Several software implementations have been reported in the literature \cite{Leo19,Liu22}. However, these implementations generally rely on a conventional SC decoding stage and invoke SCL decoding only when the initial SC decoding process fails. This strategy artificially increases the average throughput at high SNR values (e.g., $>3.0$ dB) because the computationally intensive SCL decoding is rarely executed. Nevertheless, such approaches exhibit significantly reduced throughput at low SNR, where the execution of the SCL decoder becomes necessary \cite{Leo19,Liu22}. In practice, the reported throughput on CPU and GPU platforms does not exceed a few Mbps per core, despite the use of more complex and power-hungry architectures (e.g., an RTX3090 GPU \cite{Liu22}).

A similar observation has been reported in the context of non-binary LDPC codes \cite{LeG20}. In such systems, the achievable throughput directly depends on the average number of decoding iterations required for convergence, which is strongly correlated with the SNR. Experimental evaluations performed on the $\Pl{3}$ platform using the software implementations described in \cite{LeG20} show that, for finite fields $GF=\{16,64,256\}$, a code rate of 50\%, and up to ten decoding iterations, the achieved throughput ranges from 1 to 6 Mbps in single-core mode at 0 dB when all ten iterations are effectively required. These throughput values are more than one order of magnitude lower ($>10\times$) than those reported in Section V-C of this paper, which consequently implies a similar increase in the energy consumption per decoded bit.

In the proposed approach, the computational complexity of the decoder is independent of the SNR, including in low-SNR regimes ($<0$ dB). Although a direct comparison remains difficult owing to the open and heterogeneous evaluation frameworks considered in the literature, the proposed solution enables operation in low-SNR conditions while maintaining a consistently high throughput regardless of the channel conditions.


\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This study presents the first high-throughput software decoder for \acrfull{nbp} codes, targeting modern \lpwan{} and \iot{} systems with high-order $q$-ary orthogonal modulation.
Our contributions are threefold:
\textbf{1. Algorithmic innovation:} We introduced the \acrfull{fdpfsc} algorithm, jointly processes the polar graph in the probability and frequency domains, which
By leveraging the \acrfull{fwht}, we reduced the check-node complexity from $\mathcal{O}(q^2)$ to $\mathcal{O}(q \log q)$ while efficiently handling variable-node updates.

\textbf{2. Implementation strategy:} We developed a runtime-adaptive decoder (\DecPruned{}) and a specialized generated decoder (\DecGener{}), both using \simd{}-optimized kernels and memory-efficient layouts for x86 and ARM multicore platforms.
Graph-pruning techniques (Rate-0, Rate-1, REP and SPC) further accelerate decoding with negligible performance loss.

\textbf{3. Experimental validation:} Across platforms from embedded ARM to high-end Intel servers, our decoders achieved coded throughputs from several Mbps up to 5 Gbps for \GF{16}. Performance scales nearly linearly with core count and remains practical for larger fields (\GF{64}, \GF{256}), relevant to IoT.

In conclusion, this study bridges the gap between \nbp{} theory and practical software-defined radio deployment. The decoder source code will be openly released to support adoption and further research.

A future extension of this work is the study of the suitability of \DecPruned{} and \DecGener{} variants on tightly constrained processing platforms, such as ARM and RISC-V microcontrollers, with and without vector-processing units, and exploring the use of a hybrid runtime-build time decoder, able to combine the advantages of \DecPruned{} and \DecGener{}.

Future work will address both algorithmic and application-level aspects, including extensions to list decoding and adaptive modulation schemes. In addition, an IoT application–centric and fair evaluation of the proposed approach is conducted in both LoRa and CCSK contexts, considering compliant and equivalently optimized NB-LDPC and NB-p codes.
